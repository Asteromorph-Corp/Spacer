# Water Controls Protein Droplet Formation in Cells
### Allosteric ligands modulate phase separation through hydration shell dynamics in disordered proteins

Inside our cells, proteins sometimes cluster together to form tiny droplets, much like oil forming beads in water. This process, called liquid-liquid phase separation, helps cells organize their internal components and carry out essential functions. Scientists have traditionally focused on how proteins directly interact with each other to form these droplets, but this research idea proposes a fundamentally different mechanism.

The concept centers on intrinsically disordered proteins—flexible proteins that lack a fixed shape and contain special recognition sequences that can bind to other molecules called allosteric ligands. When these ligands attach to the protein recognition motifs, the research idea suggests they may alter the surrounding water environment rather than changing the protein structure itself. Specifically, this binding could modify the dielectric properties (electrical characteristics) of the local environment and influence how water molecules arrange themselves around the protein.

This water-centered mechanism could represent a paradigm shift in understanding protein droplet formation. Instead of proteins directly grabbing onto each other to reach the critical concentration needed for droplet formation, the surrounding water environment would become the key player. By changing how water behaves around these proteins, ligand binding might lower or raise the threshold concentration required for droplets to form, effectively controlling the process through environmental changes.

If validated, this solvent-mediated approach could reshape how biologists think about cellular organization and protein condensation. Rather than viewing protein interactions as the primary driver, this research idea positions water and the local chemical environment as central controllers of when and where these crucial cellular droplets form. This perspective could open new avenues for understanding diseases linked to abnormal protein clustering and potentially lead to novel therapeutic strategies that target the water environment rather than the proteins themselves.

## Conformational shifts reshape water organization around disordered proteins

The foundation for this water-mediated control mechanism lies in the unique conformational behavior of intrinsically disordered proteins and their response to allosteric regulation. Unlike structured proteins with fixed shapes, these flexible proteins exist as dynamic ensembles of conformations that can shift in response to ligand binding [10.1021/jp511961c, 10.1371/journal.pcbi.1006393, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.5b07248, 10.1021/jacs.8b02129, 10.1126/sciadv.aax2348, 10.1021/ja710446s, 10.1073/pnas.0700329104]. When allosteric ligands bind to recognition motifs, they trigger conformational ensemble shifts that propagate through the protein's inherent conformational plasticity, fundamentally altering how the protein interacts with its aqueous environment [10.1002/pro.2304, 10.1021/ja710446s, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.1c09304, 10.1126/sciadv.aax2348, 10.1021/jacs.8b02129, 10.1371/journal.pcbi.1006393].

These conformational transitions create measurable changes in the spatial arrangement of polar and charged amino acid residues across the protein surface. As these residues redistribute, they displace the organized water molecules that form hydration shells around the protein, simultaneously altering the local electric field strength and distribution [10.1021/acs.jcim.1c01266, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.8b02440, 10.1021/ja960884f, 10.1021/ct400065j, 10.4208/cicp.300611.120911s, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.3c01683, 10.1021/ja406995j]. This reorganization directly modifies the local dielectric constant of the surrounding medium, creating a changed chemical environment that could influence intermolecular interactions necessary for phase separation.

Additionally, allosteric regulation can modulate the exposure and accessibility of short linear motifs or sticky patches within disordered proteins—regions that typically facilitate the intermolecular contacts required for droplet formation [10.1371/journal.pcbi.1006393, 10.1073/pnas.0700329104, 10.7554/elife.30688, 10.1038/s41467-023-44413-6, 10.3390/molecules25204705, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.3c01728, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.9b01731]. The theoretical frameworks established for allostery in structured proteins extend naturally to these disordered systems, where ensemble shifts can propagate through solvent-mediated mechanisms rather than through rigid structural pathways [10.7554/elife.30688, 10.1126/sciadv.aax2348, 10.1371/journal.pcbi.1006393].

## Hydration shells drive thermodynamic control of protein droplets

The thermodynamic foundation underlying this water-mediated mechanism rests on how hydration shell dynamics directly influence the energetic landscape governing protein phase transitions. When intrinsically disordered proteins undergo conformational changes in response to allosteric ligand binding, the reorganization extends beyond the protein itself to encompass the surrounding water molecules that form structured hydration shells [10.1021/acs.biomac.0c01768, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.3c02790, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.9b01731, 10.1021/jp511961c, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.5b07248, 10.3390/molecules25204705, 10.1063/5.0220861, 10.1039/d4sc05004e]. These hydration shell perturbations serve as key mediators in tuning protein interactions within aqueous environments, fundamentally altering the thermodynamic parameters that control phase separation [10.1021/jp105164t, 10.1021/jp409805p, 10.1063/1.4902822, 10.1063/1.3481089, 10.1039/d3cp03299j, 10.1002/prot.22805, 10.1155/2013/138916].

The mechanism operates through modifications to the local dielectric environment surrounding protein recognition motifs, where ligand binding events disrupt the organized water molecule networks and alter their spatial distribution [10.1063/1.2748852, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.1c00954, 10.1142/s0219633619500275, 10.1021/ja066980q, 10.1021/ci700285e, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.8b02440, 10.1039/d3cp03299j]. These changes in water organization directly impact phase separation thresholds by modifying the solvation thermodynamics of the protein system.

The theoretical basis centers on how liquid-liquid phase separation depends on the overall Gibbs free energy change, which incorporates substantial contributions from both solvent entropy and enthalpy [10.1021/acs.jpclett.2c02697, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.3c03421, 10.1039/d3cp03299j, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.9b04094, 10.1063/1.2943204, 10.1063/1.3535560, 10.1063/1.2213980]. Alterations to a protein's hydration shell directly shift its solvation free energy, thereby displacing the thermodynamic equilibrium and modifying the critical concentration threshold required for droplet formation. This solvent-mediated approach suggests that the energetic cost or benefit of removing water molecules from around proteins during clustering becomes a controllable parameter through allosteric ligand binding, potentially providing a regulatory mechanism independent of direct protein-protein contacts.

## Solvent interactions outweigh direct protein contacts in droplet formation

The energetic landscape governing protein droplet formation reveals a surprising hierarchy where solvent reorganization effects can dominate over the direct protein-protein contacts traditionally considered central to phase separation. Molecular dynamics simulations of intrinsically disordered protein condensates demonstrate that water reorganization at protein interfaces frequently contributes more significantly to the overall energetics than direct intermolecular contacts between proteins [10.1021/acs.jpcb.0c10489, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.3c02790, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.4c01153, 10.1126/sciadv.aax2348, 10.1039/d4sc05004e, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.2c06181, 10.1126/sciadv.adj2448]. This computational evidence suggests that the thermodynamic driving forces behind droplet formation are synergistically coupled with solvent dynamics, positioning water as a primary rather than secondary player in the phase separation process.

This solvent-mediated framework gains experimental support from studies using neutral osmolytes—small molecules that primarily alter bulk solvent properties like water activity without forming specific protein contacts. These osmolytes can independently induce or modulate intrinsically disordered protein phase separation, indicating that modifications to the aqueous environment alone are sufficient to shift critical concentrations [10.1039/d4sc05004e, 10.1039/c6mb00512h, 10.7554/elife.100282, 10.7554/elife.100282.3, 10.1021/acscentsci.9b00102, 10.1093/pnasnexus/pgaf039, 10.1093/jmcb/mjae038]. This experimental observation provides a direct pathway for allosteric ligands to influence phase behavior through environmental changes rather than requiring direct protein-protein bridging mechanisms.

The thermodynamic signatures supporting this mechanism can be detected using temperature-dependent isothermal titration calorimetry, which reveals significant changes in solvent entropy upon ligand binding to disordered proteins [10.1002/anie.201707853, 10.1002/ange.201707853, 10.3390/e22101187, 10.1021/acs.jcim.0c00963, 10.1021/jacsau.0c00094]. These entropy changes provide direct thermodynamic evidence that ligand-protein interactions substantially reorganize the surrounding water structure, creating conditions that could modulate phase separation thresholds through solvent-mediated pathways [10.1021/acs.jpcb.6b12814, 10.1039/c5cp07285a, 10.1021/acs.biomac.1c01301, 10.1063/1.2943204, 10.1039/d1cp03210k, 10.1002/aic.690390204, 10.7554/elife.100282].

## Biomolecules naturally regulate droplets through solvent control mechanisms

The proposed water-mediated control mechanism finds substantial support in the natural regulatory behavior of diverse cellular biomolecules that modulate phase separation through solvent-based pathways rather than direct protein interactions. Small charged biomolecules such as nucleoside triphosphates and polyamines demonstrate the capacity to drive phase separation of highly charged proteins by affecting solvent dynamics, providing direct evidence that cellular molecules routinely exploit solvent-mediated mechanisms for phase control [10.1039/d2sm00384h, 10.1021/jacs.3c09134, 10.7554/elife.64004, 10.3724/abbs.2023106].

ATP exemplifies this solvent-mediated regulatory mechanism through its dual concentration-dependent effects on protein condensation. At low concentrations, ATP-driven chaperones influence intrinsically disordered protein conformation and interaction in ways that favor phase separation via compaction and bridging mechanisms [10.1093/jmcb/mjae038, 10.1021/jacs.3c09134, 10.1126/sciadv.abm6570, 10.1021/acs.langmuir.4c04216]. Conversely, high ATP concentrations function as hydrotropes, inhibiting phase separation in intrinsically disordered proteins through solvent modification effects [10.1093/jmcb/mjae038, 10.1021/acs.langmuir.4c04216, 10.1126/sciadv.abo7885]. This concentration-dependent switching behavior demonstrates how a single biomolecule can regulate condensate formation through environmental changes rather than specific binding interactions.

The physiological relevance of this mechanism becomes apparent during cellular stress conditions, where ATP depletion—a metabolic stress indicator—promotes phase separation of certain intrinsically disordered proteins [10.1126/sciadv.abm6570, 10.1093/jmcb/mjae038, 10.1021/jacs.3c09134, 10.1021/jacsau.3c00391, 10.1126/science.aaf6846]. This stress-responsive behavior confirms that ATP acts as a biological hydrotrope to regulate condensate formation via solvent-mediated mechanisms. The coupling between protein conformational states and solvent reorganization during allosteric ligand binding provides a molecular framework explaining how these biomolecular effectors control condensate formation [10.1039/d4sc05004e, 10.3390/biology12111442, 10.1126/sciadv.aax2348, 10.1021/acs.jcim.0c00381, 10.1016/j.febslet.2014.11.028, 10.1002/pro.4375, 10.1021/acs.biochem.2c00250, 10.1002/smll.202306817]. These natural regulatory systems suggest that solvent-mediated control represents a fundamental biological strategy for managing protein droplet formation across diverse cellular contexts.

## Proven proteins validate water-mediated phase separation control

The theoretical framework for water-mediated phase separation control gains substantial validation from extensive studies of well-characterized intrinsically disordered protein systems that demonstrate allosteric regulation of phase behavior across diverse biological contexts. These established protein systems provide concrete examples where environmentally-mediated interactions, rather than direct protein-protein contacts, serve as the primary regulatory mechanism for condensate formation [10.1039/d4sc05004e, 10.1073/pnas.1706197114, 10.1126/sciadv.aax2348, 10.1063/5.0220861, 10.1126/sciadv.aax1836].

Nucleoporin assemblies exemplify this solvent-mediated control mechanism through their intrinsically disordered regions, which undergo allosteric modulation that directly affects binding affinities and phase properties [10.1126/sciadv.aax1836, 10.1002/anie.201403694, 10.1016/j.cell.2015.09.047, 10.1073/pnas.1804177115]. These nuclear pore complex components demonstrate how allosteric mechanisms in disordered regions influence protein binding to partners through environmental changes rather than structural rearrangements [10.1126/sciadv.aax1836, 10.1016/j.cell.2015.09.047]. The nucleoporin system provides direct evidence that intrinsically disordered proteins can regulate their assembly behavior through water-mediated pathways in physiologically relevant contexts.

Fused in Sarcoma protein offers another compelling example where RNA and ATP interactions modulate phase separation via allosteric-like mechanisms that operate through environmental modifications [10.1371/journal.pbio.3000327, 10.1002/prot.26335, 10.1021/acs.langmuir.3c02711, 10.1002/1873-3468.14353, 10.1126/sciadv.abo7885, 10.1038/s42003-019-0463-x, 10.11648/j.bs.20241002.13, 10.3390/ijms232113200]. Studies on yeast vacuoles and mammalian stress granules reveal that this protein undergoes regulated phase separation within cellular compartments, demonstrating the physiological relevance of solvent-mediated control mechanisms [10.1016/j.celrep.2022.111086, 10.1021/acs.langmuir.3c02711, 10.3390/ijms252212445, 10.1093/hmg/ddu243, 10.1002/1873-3468.14353, 10.1016/j.febslet.2014.11.028, 10.1096/fasebj.31.1_supplement.914.2, 10.1073/pnas.2000223117, 10.1126/science.1223728].

Tau protein isoforms provide additional validation through documented allosteric regulation that impacts phase separation relevant to neurodegenerative diseases [10.1073/pnas.2012460117, 10.1021/acschemneuro.3c00034, 10.1042/ebc20220066, 10.15252/embj.201798049, 10.1038/s41467-017-00480-0]. These disease-relevant systems demonstrate that water-mediated phase separation control operates not only in normal cellular processes but also in pathological contexts where dysregulated protein condensation contributes to disease progression. The convergent evidence from these diverse protein systems suggests that solvent-mediated allosteric control represents a fundamental mechanism governing intrinsically disordered protein phase behavior across multiple biological contexts.

## Cutting-Edge Tools Reveal Water's Hidden Role

The proposed water-mediated phase separation mechanism can be rigorously tested and validated using sophisticated experimental and computational approaches that provide both molecular-level insights and quantitative measurements of phase behavior. These advanced techniques offer the precision necessary to detect and characterize the subtle environmental changes that drive this solvent-mediated regulatory system.

Dielectric spectroscopy emerges as a particularly powerful tool for directly measuring the local environmental changes proposed in this mechanism. Operating across the megahertz-to-terahertz frequency range, this technique can detect alterations in local dielectric properties and hydration dynamics within protein solutions as allosteric ligands bind to recognition motifs [10.1117/12.2567857, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.8b02872, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.6b01491, 10.1021/ma051854c, 10.1021/jp049618b]. By monitoring how the electrical properties of the surrounding water change in response to ligand binding, researchers can obtain real-time evidence for the proposed hydration shell reorganization that underlies this regulatory mechanism.

Complementing experimental approaches, all-atom molecular dynamics simulations provide unprecedented molecular-level detail about the energetic contributions driving protein condensate formation. These computational studies can systematically deconstruct the various forces involved in protein self-association, revealing how water reorganization at protein interfaces often contributes more significantly to phase behavior than direct protein-protein contacts [10.1021/acs.jpcb.0c10489, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.3c02790, 10.1021/acs.jpclett.4c01153, 10.1126/sciadv.aax2348, 10.1039/d4sc05004e, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.2c06181, 10.1126/sciadv.adj2448]. This computational evidence confirms that phase behavior operates through synergistic coupling with solvent dynamics, supporting the theoretical foundation for water-mediated control.

Perhaps most importantly for validating this mechanism, quantitative phase diagram measurements enable precise mapping of the protein concentration and temperature conditions that support liquid-liquid phase separation. These systematic studies can directly quantify how specific ligand concentrations shift the critical concentration thresholds required for droplet formation, providing unambiguous evidence for environmentally-mediated phase control [10.1073/pnas.84.20.7079, 10.1073/pnas.212507199, 10.1039/c2sm07008a, 10.1073/pnas.242746499]. The combination of these experimental and computational approaches creates a comprehensive toolkit for investigating how water and the local chemical environment control protein condensation, potentially revealing a fundamental biological regulatory mechanism that has been overlooked in traditional studies focused solely on protein-protein interactions.
