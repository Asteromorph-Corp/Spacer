# DNA Structures May Control Brain Development and Disease
### Intronic G-quadruplex topology influences splice site selection through RNA polymerase pausing

This research idea explores a fascinating connection between DNA structure and brain development by investigating how specific DNA formations called G-quadruplexes might control which parts of genes get included in the final protein blueprints. G-quadruplexes are special four-stranded DNA structures that form when certain DNA sequences fold back on themselves, creating stable but unusual shapes within the genetic code.

The concept suggests that these G-quadruplex structures, when located in the non-coding regions of genes (called introns), might act like molecular roadblocks that influence how genes are processed in developing brain cells. During normal gene expression, a cellular machine called the spliceosome cuts out introns and joins together the protein-coding segments (exons) to create the final messenger RNA. This research proposes that G-quadruplex structures could physically interfere with this splicing process by causing RNA polymerase II, the enzyme that reads genes, to pause or slow down at specific locations.

What makes this idea particularly compelling is its potential connection to brain diseases that emerge during development. The research suggests that when G-quadruplex structures create abnormal splicing patterns, they might cause incorrect pieces of genetic information to be included in the final protein instructions. This could lead to the production of faulty proteins that contribute to neurodevelopmental disorders.

By systematically mapping where these G-quadruplex structures occur and understanding their specific shapes and orientations, researchers could potentially identify new mechanisms underlying brain development disorders. This approach might reveal why certain genetic regions are particularly vulnerable to producing disease-causing protein variants, offering fresh insights into conditions that affect brain development and potentially opening new avenues for understanding neurological diseases.

## G-quadruplexes possess ideal biophysical properties for neuronal regulation

The remarkable biophysical characteristics of G-quadruplexes position them as exceptionally well-suited regulatory elements for the precise temporal control required during neuronal development. These structures demonstrate extraordinary thermal stability, with melting temperatures frequently exceeding 50°C, ensuring their persistence under the physiological conditions present in developing brain tissue [10.1021/acsomega.7b01649, 10.1021/ja064536h, 10.1021/ac102168m]. This thermal resilience suggests that G-quadruplexes can maintain their regulatory function throughout the extended timeframes characteristic of neuronal differentiation processes.

RNA G-quadruplexes exhibit even greater stability than their DNA counterparts when exposed to conditions that mimic cellular environments, enhancing their potential as persistent regulatory switches during transcription and splicing events [10.1021/bi1002822, 10.1021/jacs.6b04506, 10.1093/nar/gkaa1126]. This increased stability in RNA form is particularly relevant given that G-quadruplexes would form in nascent RNA transcripts during gene expression.

The kinetic properties of G-quadruplex formation align remarkably well with the temporal requirements of transcriptional regulation. Biophysical measurements reveal that these structures fold and unfold on millisecond to minute timescales, precisely matching the duration of RNA polymerase pausing events observed during transcription [10.1021/ja309851t, 10.1021/acs.jpcb.0c02548, 10.1021/acs.biochem.4c00601]. Additionally, their polymorphic nature allows them to adopt different conformations in response to changing cellular conditions, providing a mechanism for adaptive splicing regulation as neurons develop [10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.1093/carcin/bgu206, 10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1021/bi1002822, 10.7554/elife.17820]. Single-molecule techniques have confirmed these rapid resolution kinetics, supporting their compatibility with dynamic transcriptional processes in neuronal cells [10.1093/nar/gks1341, 10.1021/ja4019176, 10.1073/pnas.2433350100, 10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.1523/jneurosci.0093-23.2024, 10.1101/gad.305862.117].

## Developmental windows create unique G-quadruplex vulnerability in neurons

The regulatory influence of G-quadruplexes on neuronal gene expression appears to be significantly amplified during specific developmental stages, creating temporal windows of heightened vulnerability to splicing dysregulation. This heightened sensitivity stems from the stage-specific expression patterns of key helicase enzymes, particularly DHX36, which normally function to resolve G-quadruplex structures and maintain proper transcriptional flow [10.1523/jneurosci.0093-23.2024, 10.1038/s41467-019-10432-5, 10.1101/gad.211243.112, 10.1111/gtc.13061, 10.1002/ana.20379, 10.3389/fnins.2023.1116087].

Evidence from neuronal systems demonstrates that G-quadruplexes function as persistent transcriptional control devices, maintaining regulatory influence over extended periods during critical processes such as memory consolidation and cellular stress responses [10.1523/jneurosci.0093-23.2024, 10.1038/s41467-021-22129-9, 10.18632/aging.101282]. This extended regulatory capability suggests that these structures could accumulate and exert prolonged effects on splicing patterns during the lengthy timeframes characteristic of neuronal differentiation.

The functional significance of G-quadruplex-mediated regulation becomes particularly evident through studies using G-quadruplex stabilizing compounds, which demonstrate the ability to selectively downregulate specific neuronal genes [10.18632/aging.101282, 10.7554/elife.52283, 10.1021/acschemneuro.0c00194, 10.1523/jneurosci.0093-23.2024]. These findings establish a direct mechanistic link between G-quadruplex structure stabilization and altered gene expression outcomes in neural cells, supporting the concept that these formations serve as functional regulatory switches rather than merely incidental structural features.

The developmental vulnerability hypothesis gains further support from observations that DHX36 dysregulation correlates with neurodegenerative conditions, indicating that proper G-quadruplex resolution is essential for maintaining neuronal transcriptomic integrity throughout development and maturation [10.1523/jneurosci.0093-23.2024, 10.1038/s41467-019-10432-5]. This suggests that when helicase activity is insufficient during critical developmental periods, G-quadruplexes could persist and disrupt normal splicing patterns, potentially contributing to neurodevelopmental disorders.

## Physical barriers create temporal windows for splicing control

The mechanistic foundation for G-quadruplex-mediated splicing regulation rests on their capacity to function as transient physical barriers during transcription, creating precisely timed opportunities for spliceosome reorganization. These stable secondary structures arise from guanine-rich sequences that fold into four-stranded configurations, presenting substantial physical impediments that RNA polymerase II must navigate during gene transcription [10.1016/j.molcel.2010.11.005, 10.1186/s12915-025-02229-4, 10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1093/nar/gkaa1126].

High-resolution structural studies have revealed the intricate nature of these barriers, demonstrating that G-quadruplexes can form dimeric configurations and engage in competitive interactions with complementary DNA strands, establishing complex regulatory networks that could influence splice site accessibility [10.1371/journal.pcbi.1007383, 10.1093/nar/gkj421, 10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.7554/elife.17820]. When RNA polymerase II encounters these structures in nascent transcripts, the resulting elongation pause represents more than a simple transcriptional delay—it creates a critical temporal window during which splicing machinery can assemble with altered kinetics.

The pausing mechanism operates through direct physical obstruction, as G-quadruplex structures present formidable barriers that require active resolution before transcription can proceed [10.1186/s12915-025-02229-4, 10.1073/pnas.1205063109]. This enforced pause provides extended time frames that fundamentally alter the recruitment dynamics of spliceosome components and RNA-binding proteins at adjacent splice sites [10.1016/j.molcel.2021.02.016, 10.1038/s41467-019-08381-0, 10.1016/j.molcel.2010.11.005, 10.1016/j.molcel.2018.09.004]. These temporal modifications could shift the competitive balance between alternative splice sites, potentially favoring the inclusion or exclusion of specific exons based on the proximity and strength of nearby G-quadruplex structures. The resulting splicing alterations may represent a previously unrecognized layer of post-transcriptional control that could be particularly significant during periods of rapid transcriptomic change in developing neurons.

## G-quadruplexes recruit protein partners to orchestrate splicing decisions

The structural complexity of G-quadruplexes extends beyond their role as transcriptional barriers to encompass a sophisticated protein recruitment function that could fundamentally reshape splicing landscapes in developing neurons. These intricate three-dimensional formations create specialized binding surfaces that selectively attract specific RNA-binding proteins, transforming G-quadruplex-containing transcripts into dynamic platforms for splicing regulation [10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.7554/elife.17820, 10.1093/nar/gku290].

The architectural features of G-quadruplexes establish distinct topological environments that favor the recruitment of particular RNA-binding proteins, including members of the heterogeneous nuclear ribonucleoprotein (hnRNP) family such as hnRNP F and hnRNP H. These proteins function as molecular switches that can either enhance or suppress the recognition of nearby splice sites, depending on their specific binding patterns and cellular concentrations [10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1371/journal.pbio.0040021, 10.1093/nar/22.6.1059, 10.1074/jbc.m700774200]. The selectivity of this recruitment process suggests that G-quadruplexes operate as molecular beacons, concentrating specific regulatory factors at critical decision points during pre-mRNA processing.

Advanced proteomic approaches, particularly co-immunoprecipitation coupled with mass spectrometry, have begun to reveal the extensive network of proteins that associate with G-quadruplex structures in cellular contexts. These investigations identify both enhancer and suppressor proteins that could amplify or mitigate the splicing disruptions initiated by G-quadruplex formation [10.1093/nar/gku290, 10.7554/elife.17820, 10.1021/acs.jproteome.1c00603, 10.1016/j.dib.2018.02.081, 10.1101/gad.305862.117]. The discovery of this protein interaction network indicates that G-quadruplexes may function as central organizing hubs that coordinate multiple layers of splicing control, potentially explaining how these structures could exert disproportionate influence on neurodevelopmental gene expression programs through the recruitment of tissue-specific regulatory factors.

## Mutations create pathogenic G-quadruplexes disrupting neuronal splice regulation

The pathological significance of G-quadruplex-mediated splicing disruption gains substantial support from emerging evidence that disease-causing mutations in neurodevelopmental disorder genes directly create or stabilize these regulatory structures. Analysis of de novo mutations associated with autism spectrum disorder reveals a concerning pattern where genetic alterations in critical neuronal genes generate novel G-quadruplex-forming sequences that subsequently disrupt normal splicing patterns [10.1039/c7mb00360a, 10.1002/ajmg.b.30822, 10.1038/s41525-021-00254-0, 10.1371/journal.pone.0116358, 10.1186/2040-2392-5-30, 10.1039/c7mb00360a].

The mechanistic basis for this pathogenic effect centers on the ability of G-quadruplex structures to fundamentally alter the co-transcriptional splicing process through coordinated disruption of multiple regulatory layers. When G-quadruplexes form in intronic regions, they create temporal windows during RNA polymerase pausing that shift the recruitment dynamics of RNA-binding proteins, ultimately determining whether specific exons are included or excluded from mature transcripts [10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.1093/carcin/bgq253, 10.1021/acschembio.0c00260, 10.1093/nar/26.24.5568, 10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1093/nar/gkaa1126]. This topological influence represents a previously underappreciated mechanism through which intronic G-quadruplex structures can function as molecular switches that govern splice site selection in developing neurons.

The clinical relevance of this mechanism becomes evident through specific examples where mutations in neurodevelopmentally important genes create pathogenic G-quadruplex structures. Mutations in the GRIN1 promoter region demonstrate how genetic alterations can establish G-quadruplex-forming sequences that potentially disrupt normal gene expression patterns. Similarly, splice site mutations in SHANK3, a gene with established causal links to autism spectrum disorder, illustrate how G-quadruplex formation could contribute to the pathological splicing patterns observed in neurodevelopmental conditions [10.1039/c7mb00360a, 10.1002/ajmg.b.30822, 10.1038/s41525-021-00254-0, 10.1371/journal.pone.0116358, 10.1186/2040-2392-5-30]. These findings suggest that the disruption of exon inclusion and exclusion balance through G-quadruplex-induced alterations may represent a common pathway through which diverse genetic mutations contribute to neurodevelopmental pathology [10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.7554/elife.17820, 10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1111/j.1471-4159.2012.07680.x].

## Evolutionary conservation validates G-quadruplexes as therapeutic targets

The evolutionary persistence of G-quadruplex motifs across diverse species provides substantial evidence for their functional importance in neuronal gene regulation and reveals promising therapeutic opportunities for treating neurodevelopmental disorders. Comparative genomic analysis across eleven mammalian and avian species demonstrates a remarkable conservation pattern, with G-quadruplex-forming sequences showing significant enrichment near splice junctions throughout vertebrate lineages [10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7]. This conservation pattern extends far beyond what would be expected by chance, indicating that natural selection has actively maintained these structures due to their critical regulatory functions in alternative splicing control.

The therapeutic potential of targeting disease-associated G-quadruplexes has already been demonstrated through the development of selective small molecule inhibitors. These compounds show remarkable specificity for pathogenic G-quadruplex structures, such as those found in the C9orf72 gene associated with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis and frontotemporal dementia. When tested in cellular and animal models, these therapeutic molecules successfully ameliorate the pathological signatures characteristic of these devastating neurological conditions [10.1021/acs.jmedchem.2c00654, 10.1002/cbic.202400974, 10.1021/acschemneuro.1c00470, 10.1038/srep01016, 10.1038/s41467-023-43872-1, 10.3390/molecules28124671, 10.1073/pnas.2210532119, 10.3390/brainsci11111543, 10.1126/scitranslmed.abd5991, 10.1038/s41467-021-21112-8, 10.1093/hmg/ddac298, 10.15252/emmm.201707850, 10.1038/srep16673].

The convergence of evolutionary conservation data with successful therapeutic interventions suggests that G-quadruplex-targeting approaches could represent a broadly applicable strategy for treating neurodevelopmental disorders. The fact that these structures have been maintained across millions of years of evolution, combined with their demonstrated druggability in disease models, positions G-quadruplex biology as a particularly promising avenue for developing precision therapies that could address the fundamental splicing dysregulation underlying various neurological conditions.

## Mapping G-quadruplex structures reveals splicing disruption mechanisms

The experimental feasibility of investigating G-quadruplex-mediated splicing regulation is strongly supported by the availability of sophisticated computational and experimental tools that can precisely map these structures and validate their functional consequences. Advanced computational algorithms, particularly G4Hunter, provide robust prediction capabilities for identifying G-quadruplex-forming sequences within genes associated with neurodevelopmental disorders, establishing a systematic foundation for empirical validation of their splicing impacts [10.1093/nar/gkw006, 10.1093/bioinformatics/btz087, 10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7]. These computational approaches enable systematic mapping of G-quadruplex formation potential across disease-associated genes, facilitating correlation of structural features with aberrant exon inclusion patterns observed in neurodevelopmental conditions [10.1093/nar/gkae797, 10.7554/elife.17820, 10.1038/srep01016, 10.1093/nar/gkae473, 10.1126/sciadv.abd9440].

The mechanistic validation of G-quadruplex effects on splicing can be achieved through complementary biochemical and cellular approaches that directly demonstrate causality. Biochemical footprinting techniques provide definitive evidence for G-quadruplex formation in specific RNA sequences, establishing the structural basis for functional splicing alterations [10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.7554/elife.17820, 10.1038/s41467-018-07224-8, 10.1002/anie.201500891]. Minigene assays offer particularly compelling experimental validation by directly testing G-quadruplex effects on splice site selection, providing causal evidence for their role in determining exon inclusion patterns [10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.1101/gad.305862.117].

The developmental relevance of G-quadruplex-mediated splicing regulation can be effectively modeled using induced pluripotent stem cell-derived neuronal systems, which recapitulate critical neurodevelopmental stages and enable observation of G-quadruplex-mediated splicing changes over 7-21 day differentiation periods [10.4172/1747-0862.1000122, 10.21769/bioprotoc.3188, 10.1073/pnas.1202944109, 10.1073/pnas.0910012107, 10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.1038/s41467-018-07224-8, 10.1073/pnas.0910012107, 10.3389/fcell.2021.727747, 10.3389/fcell.2022.1023340]. Genome-wide mapping techniques further enhance this experimental framework by identifying comprehensive G-quadruplex landscapes within the transcriptome, revealing their correlation with regulatory hotspots in neurodevelopmental genes [10.1080/21541264.2016.1243505, 10.1186/1756-0500-5-138, 10.1093/nar/gkw011, 10.1093/nar/gkp740, 10.1021/acschemneuro.0c00194]. Single-cell profiling techniques add another dimension by mapping G-quadruplex structures at high fidelity, revealing the variability in their contributions to pathological splicing across individual cells [10.1038/s41598-021-02943-3, 10.1038/s41467-022-30071-7, 10.1101/gad.305862.117, 10.7554/elife.17820, 10.1038/s41467-018-07224-8].
